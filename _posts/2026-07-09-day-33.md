what_i_learned: |

   Today, our multi-dataset evaluation pipeline expanded significantly as I integrated our third major clinical data stream: the MIMIC Dataset. Following our strategic evaluation plan, I structured experiments focusing strictly on ECG and PPG signals only, intentionally omitting the respiratory (RESP) channel for this phase. This setup leaves the RESP signal safely in reserve to serve as a tiebreaker and arbitration domain for our final multimodal downstream experiments. Using our established research manual protocol, I ran manual-style benign and non-benign perturbations across the MIMIC waveforms. From these perturbed signals, I executed feature extraction pipelines to capture three distinct representations: Time Domain features, Frequency Domain features, Time-Frequency Domain (spectrogram/wavelet) representations. Once the multi-domain feature vectors were embedded, I deployed the pyCFD library to run same-encoder discovery reports. This allowed me to rigorously audit how the structural boundaries of the shared latent space respond to multi-domain signals. Following the step-by-step experiment layout inside our Overleaf research manual, I generated a new suite of comparative plots. This allows us to begin the final phase of our research: a comprehensive, side-by-side comparison across all three of our core datasets PTB-XL, BIDMC, and MIMIC. Evaluating everything from individual complementary feature pairs up to our final multimodal model predictions.

reflection: |

   Reflecting on today's work, seeing our pipeline successfully ingest and process the MIMIC dataset is incredibly rewarding. Scaling an architecture from PTB-XL and BIDMC over to a massive, real-world clinical data source like MIMIC is the ultimate stress test for a machine learning model. It forced me to be highly precise with data dimensions and sampling rates, making sure our feature extractions across the time and frequency domains were aligned. The decision to hold back the respiratory signal as an "arbitration domain" is an architectural choice. In a lot of the literature I reviewed earlier this week, models throw every available signal into the fusion layer immediately. Which makes it hard to pinpoint exactly where an adversarial or noisy signal is breaking the system. By saving RESP as a tiebreaker, we are building a model that can genuinely handle conflicting information safely. Seeing the pyCFD same-encoder discovery results start to populate on Overleaf alongside our existing PTB-XL and BIDMC baselines gives me total confidence. We are no longer just looking at numbers on a screen; we are looking at a cross-validated framework that stands up to diverse clinical noise. The workflow on Overleaf is becoming second nature, and the final paper is starting to come together.
